%=============================================================
\section{Two Traditions, One Problem}
\label{sec:traditions}
%=============================================================

Hardware security mechanisms are evaluated twice, by two communities that
rarely compare notes. Systems engineers evaluate them as points in a design
space: what do they cost in silicon area, power draw, and runtime performance,
and what do those costs foreclose? Security economists evaluate them as
instruments of policy: who adopts them, who pays for them, and who bears the
risk when they fail? Both evaluations are rigorous. Both are incomplete. The
gap between them is precisely where the fog of war lives.

%-------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Systems Engineering Lens: Everything is a Tradeoff}
\label{sec:engineering}
%-------------------------------------------------------------

Hardware designers operate under a foundational constraint: resources are
finite and their allocation is zero-sum. Silicon area committed to a security
feature is area unavailable for compute cores or cache. Power budgeted to
always-on integrity checking is power unavailable for performance. Every
mechanism comes with a profile---not a single cost, but a vector of costs
across multiple dimensions and lifecycle phases: die area and verification
burden at design time; workload interaction and activation conditions at
deployment time; patch obligations and certification renewals at lifecycle time.

This framework has produced a substantial body of precise empirical
work~\cite{costan2016sanctum,tsai2017graphenesgx,koruyeh2022retbleed,yan2018invisispec,arm2021mte,woodruff2014cheri}.
Hardware security researchers know, with considerable precision, what their
mechanisms cost in engineering terms. What the framework does not track is
\emph{who bears those costs}. Distribution is not a variable in design space
exploration. It is treated as someone else's problem, to be worked out
downstream by contracts and markets the engineer neither controls nor models.

%-------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Security Economics Lens: Distribution is the Point}
\label{sec:economics}
%-------------------------------------------------------------

Security economics starts not from the mechanism but from the actors---their
incentives, their information, and the ways individually rational decisions
produce collectively suboptimal outcomes. The core insight is that security
is an economic problem before it is a technical one~\cite{anderson2001why}:
when the actor who decides to invest in a control is not the actor who bears
the cost of a breach, underinvestment is the predictable equilibrium. When
buyers cannot verify security properties before purchase, markets trend toward
insecurity~\cite{akerlof1970lemons}. When failures impose costs on parties
outside the original decision chain, the gap between private and social
returns to security investment widens~\cite{anderson2006economics}.

In hardware security specifically, the economics lens illuminates patterns
technical analysis cannot. DRAM vendors initially resisted rowhammer
mitigations because vulnerability costs fell on system integrators, not on
vendors whose products were already sold~\cite{kim2014flipping,frigo2020trrespass}.
OEMs ship platforms with security features disabled because integration costs
fall on the OEM while benefits accrue to end users. Firmware security is
chronically underfunded because compromise is hard to attribute and liability
is diffuse.

What the economics lens does not provide is a way to trace these distributional
outcomes back to specific technical properties of the mechanisms involved. The
mechanism is a parameter---present or absent---rather than an object of analysis.
Why rowhammer mitigations concentrate costs on system integrators rather than
DRAM vendors, and whether alternative designs would have implied a different
distribution, is not a question the economics toolkit can answer. Those answers
are embedded in the mechanisms themselves. To see them, you need the engineering
lens. To know why they matter, you need the economics lens.

%-------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Gap: A Single Mechanism, Two Descriptions}
\label{sec:gap}
%-------------------------------------------------------------

To make the gap concrete, consider hardware AES acceleration---a dedicated
on-chip engine present in most modern processors.

A hardware architect would describe it thus: the accelerator occupies a few
percent of SoC die area, delivers ten-to-forty times the throughput of software
AES~\cite{intel2012aesni,xie2024opentitan}, but only for workloads that invoke
it---applications that perform little encryption see no benefit and pay only
the area and static power cost. Its opportunity cost is specific: that die area
is unavailable for a larger cache or additional CPU core, both of which would
benefit all workloads. The chip vendor including this accelerator is making a
commitment about which workload class deserves dedicated silicon.

A security economist would describe the same mechanism entirely differently:
the accelerator changes the cost structure of encryption for downstream actors,
lowering the barrier to adoption for integrators who previously avoided
encryption on performance grounds---a positive externality the chip vendor
bears the cost of but does not fully capture. It also creates a principal-agent
problem: the vendor does not bear the consequence if applications fail to use
it. And it shifts responsibility for cryptographic correctness toward software,
since the hardware is fast but correctness depends on key management, protocol
selection, and algorithm choice---all now the application developer's problem.

These are two precise, sophisticated descriptions of the same object. They do
not conflict. They do not connect.

The architect's description has no place for ``positive externality,''
``principal-agent dynamic,'' or ``responsibility transfer.'' The economist's
has no place for ``activation profile,'' ``opportunity cost of die area,'' or
``workload-conditional benefit.'' Each tradition has exactly the vocabulary it
needs for its own purposes, and that vocabulary stops at the other tradition's
boundary.

The consequence is that the most important questions about hardware AES
acceleration cannot be asked within either framework alone. \emph{Why} does
the vendor bear the area cost while the security benefit accrues to end users?
Because the activation profile is conditional and scope is module-specific---
properties invisible to the economics lens. \emph{What} would a different
design imply? An always-on engine with mandatory invocation would shift benefit
realization toward the vendor's customers---but evaluating that requires
simultaneously reasoning about technical properties and distributional
consequences in a way neither tradition currently supports.

This is the gap the present paper addresses: not a gap in either tradition's
sophistication, but a gap between them. The following sections develop the
translation layer inductively, through case studies chosen to make the gap---
and its resolution---as concrete as possible.
